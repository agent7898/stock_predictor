SECTION 1 — PROBLEM STATEMENT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1.1 BACKGROUND
Stock price prediction is the attempt to determine the future value of a company's stock or other financial instrument traded on an exchange. It is a critical task in finance because accurate predictions can lead to significant financial gain, while errors can result in substantial losses. Stock prices are influenced by countless factors, making them notoriously difficult to predict.
Financial time series data—like daily stock prices—is inherently noisy and non-stationary. Unlike physical systems that obey fixed laws, financial markets are driven by human behavior, macroeconomic indicators, company performance, geopolitical events, and market sentiment. This means the underlying statistical properties of the data, such as its mean and variance, change over time. Time series analysis in this context means analyzing a sequence of historical data points, ordered chronologically, to extract meaningful statistics and characteristics, and using these patterns to forecast future values.

1.2 THE CORE PROBLEM
Given a historical sequence of daily closing prices for a publicly traded stock, predict the likely price movement over a future horizon of N days.
In this project, the problem is treated as a regression task, not a classification task. Regression aims to predict the exact continuous numerical value (the exact price), whereas classification would only predict the direction (up or down). We use regression because we want to quantify the magnitude of the predicted movement and evaluate the algorithms based on how close their predicted prices are to the actual prices.

1.3 WHY THIS IS DIFFICULT
- Random walk hypothesis: This hypothesis suggests that stock market prices evolve according to a random walk and thus cannot be predicted. If prices instantly reflect all available information, future price changes are independent of past prices, making forecasting fundamentally impossible.
- Overfitting risk: Complex machine learning models, especially Neural Networks, possess high variance and can easily memorize the noise in the historical data instead of learning the underlying signal. This results in models that perform exceptionally well on past data but fail catastrophically on unseen future data.
- Non-stationarity: Statistical properties such as mean, variance, and autocorrelation change over time. Thus, the rules a model learns about the market today might become entirely invalid tomorrow as market regimes shift.
- Lagged effects: Today's price is influenced by events weeks ago. Some market reactions are instantaneous, while others unfold slowly over weeks or months, meaning the model must decide how far back in time to look for relevant patterns.
- Limited data: Daily prices give only ~250 points per year. Even a 5-year dataset contains around 1,250 points. For complex models like Deep Learning, this is considered a very small dataset, increasing the risk of overfitting.

1.4 WHAT THIS PROJECT DOES
This project builds a system that:
- Sources real historical data from Yahoo Finance
- Applies three different algorithmic approaches to the same data
- Evaluates each approach using MAE, RMSE, and R-squared metrics
- Visualizes predictions and future forecasts in a web dashboard
- Computes market sentiment using technical indicators
The goal of this project is comparative analysis of algorithms. It is designed to demonstrate how different predictive models perform on identical noisy time series data. It is NOT intended to provide production trading advice.

1.5 SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
- The daily closing price is the only input feature used for predictions.
- No news, earnings reports, macroeconomic indicators, or sentiment data is used as inputs to the prediction models.
- Predictions beyond 10-15 days carry high uncertainty due to the compounding nature of recursive forecasting.
- The model is retrained fresh on every prediction request; no pre-trained weights are saved or reused across requests.

2.5 PREPROCESSING STEPS
Step 1 — NaN removal:
  yfinance occasionally returns missing values for holidays or data gaps. All rows containing NaN in any column are dropped to ensure clean inputs.

Step 2 — MinMax Scaling (for LR and LSTM only):
  Formula: x_scaled = (x - x_min) / (x_max - x_min)
  Result: all values mapped to range [0.0, 1.0]
  Why: neural networks and gradient-based models converge faster and more stably when inputs are in a small, uniform range.
  Important: scaler is fit ONLY on training data, then applied to test data — this prevents data leakage.
  ARIMA does NOT use scaling — it operates on raw prices.

Step 3 — Train/Test Split:
  80% of data used for training.
  20% of data used for testing.
  Split is chronological — no shuffling.
  For 502 data points: ~401 train, ~101 test.

Step 4 — Sequence Creation (for LR and LSTM):
  Time series data is converted into supervised learning sequences using a sliding window approach, generating input features (X) and target outputs (y). Explained in detail under each algorithm section.

2.6 SUPPORTED TICKERS
Any valid Yahoo Finance ticker symbol is supported.
Examples: AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, TSLA, NVDA, AMZN
Indian stocks use .NS suffix: RELIANCE.NS, TCS.NS, INFY.NS

3.1 OVERALL PIPELINE
1. Raw prices array (np.float64) enters the pipeline
2. Each model independently processes the same prices array
3. Each model returns a standardized dict with the same keys
4. app.py collects all three results and aligns test date windows
5. Results are serialized to JSON and sent to the frontend

3.2 SHARED DESIGN DECISIONS
Decision 1 — Fresh instantiation per request:
  A new model object is created for every prediction request. No state is shared between requests. Why: prevents contamination between different tickers or periods.

Decision 2 — Fixed 80/20 chronological split:
  The split index = int(len(prices) * 0.8)
  Training always uses older data, testing always uses newer data. Why: simulates real-world scenario where future is unknown.

Decision 3 — Metrics computed on inverse-transformed values:
  All MAE, RMSE, R-squared values are computed on original price scale (e.g. dollars), not on scaled 0-1 values. Why: scaled metrics are meaningless to interpret.

Decision 4 — Iterative forecasting:
  For future N-day forecast, each model predicts one step, appends that prediction to the input window, then predicts the next step. This is called recursive multi-step forecasting. Limitation: errors compound over each step, causing increasing uncertainty the further into the future you predict.

3.3 WHAT EACH MODEL RETURNS
  test_actual    — list of real historical prices from test period (inverse transformed back to dollar values)
  test_predicted — list of model's predictions for the test period (inverse transformed back to dollar values)
  forecast       — list of predicted prices for future N days
  mae            — Mean Absolute Error in dollars
  rmse           — Root Mean Squared Error in dollars
  r2             — R-squared (Linear Regression only)
  order          — best (p,d,q) found by AIC search (ARIMA only)

3.4 ALIGNMENT LOGIC IN APP.PY
Because ARIMA uses rolling validation (capped at 60 steps) and LSTM loses look_back points from the front of its test set, the three models produce test arrays of different lengths. To render them on the same chart, shorter arrays are front-padded with None values to match the length of the LR test array.

3.5 SENTIMENT ANALYSIS LOGIC
This project includes a rule-based scoring system in utils/sentiment.py.
5 technical indicators are computed from the last 90 days:
  RSI (14-day): measures overbought/oversold condition
  SMA crossover: 20-day vs 50-day moving average
  10-day momentum: percentage price change over 10 days
  52-week proximity: how close price is to its yearly high
  Volatility: annualized standard deviation of daily returns
Each indicator contributes +1, -1, or +2, -2 to a score. The score maps to labels from Strongly Bullish to Strongly Bearish. No external news or NLP is used — purely price-derived.

4.1 LINEAR REGRESSION
------------------------------------------------------

4.1.1 WHAT IT IS
Linear Regression fits a straight line (or hyperplane) through data points. The line minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances between each point and the line (least squares criterion). In this project, it is used as a multi-variate regression: 30 past prices (features) → 1 future price (target). This is called a lag-feature approach or sliding window approach.

4.1.2 THE MATHEMATICS
Prediction formula:
  y_hat = w1*x1 + w2*x2 + ... + w30*x30 + b
Where:
  x1 to x30 = the last 30 closing prices (scaled)
  w1 to w30 = learned weights
  b         = bias term
  y_hat     = predicted next price (scaled)

Weights are learned by minimizing the sum of (y_actual - y_predicted)^2.
This has a closed-form solution: w = (X^T X)^-1 X^T y. No iteration needed — solved in one step.

Metrics formulas:
  MAE  = (1/n) * sum(|y_actual_i - y_pred_i|)
  RMSE = sqrt((1/n) * sum((y_actual_i - y_pred_i)^2))
  R2   = 1 - (sum((y_actual - y_pred)^2) / sum((y_actual - mean(y_actual))^2))

4.1.3 HOW IT IS USED IN THIS PROJECT
In linear_model.py:
  - look_back = 30 (window size)
  - scaler.fit_transform on training prices only
  - _make_sequences creates X of shape (n, 30) and y of shape (n,)
  - Each row of X is 30 consecutive scaled prices
  - Corresponding y is the price at position 31
  - sklearn LinearRegression().fit(X_train, y_train)
  - Predictions inverse_transformed before metrics
  - Iterative forecast: predict step N, append to window, repeat

Code snippet from models/linear_model.py:
```python
    def _make_sequences(self, arr):
        X, y = [], []
        for i in range(self.look_back, len(arr)):
            X.append(arr[i - self.look_back:i, 0])
            y.append(arr[i, 0])
        return np.array(X), np.array(y)
```

4.1.4 WORKED EXAMPLE
Small dataset of 7 real-style AAPL closing prices:
  Day 1: 226.43
  Day 2: 224.18
  Day 3: 220.79
  Day 4: 221.55
  Day 5: 221.89
  Day 6: 225.77
  Day 7: 229.04

Using look_back = 3 for this example.

Step 1 — Scaling:
  min = 220.79, max = 229.04
  Day 1: (226.43 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 0.683
  Day 2: (224.18 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 0.411
  Day 3: (220.79 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 0.000
  Day 4: (221.55 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 0.092
  Day 5: (221.89 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 0.133
  Day 6: (225.77 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 0.603
  Day 7: (229.04 - 220.79) / (229.04 - 220.79) = 1.000

Step 2 — Create sequences (look_back=3):
  X[0] = [0.683, 0.411, 0.000],  y[0] = 0.092  (predict Day 4)
  X[1] = [0.411, 0.000, 0.092],  y[1] = 0.133  (predict Day 5)
  X[2] = [0.000, 0.092, 0.133],  y[2] = 0.603  (predict Day 6)
  X[3] = [0.092, 0.133, 0.603],  y[3] = 1.000  (predict Day 7)

Step 3 — Train (use X[0],X[1],X[2] as train, X[3] as test):
  Since prices are trending up, weights for more recent days will be larger than weights for older days.
  Assume learned weights after fitting: w=[0.1, 0.3, 0.8], b=0.05

Step 4 — Predict Day 7:
  y_hat = 0.1*0.092 + 0.3*0.133 + 0.8*0.603 + 0.05
        = 0.0092 + 0.0399 + 0.4824 + 0.05
        = 0.5815

Step 5 — Inverse transform:
  price = 0.5815 * (229.04 - 220.79) + 220.79
        = 0.5815 * 8.25 + 220.79
        = 4.797 + 220.79
        = 225.59

  Actual Day 7 = 229.04, Predicted = 225.59
  Error = |229.04 - 225.59| = 3.45

Step 6 — Forecast Day 8 (iterative):
  Take last 3 known scaled values: [0.133, 0.603, 1.000]
  y_hat = 0.1*0.133 + 0.3*0.603 + 0.8*1.000 + 0.05 = 1.044
  Clamp to [0,1]: 1.000 (capped at max)
  Inverse: 1.000 * 8.25 + 220.79 = 229.04
  Forecast Day 8 price: $229.04

Strengths: Extremely fast, explainable, provides a solid baseline.
Weaknesses: Cannot capture non-linear relationships. Heavily dependent on window size.

4.2 ARIMA
------------------------------------------------------

4.2.1 WHAT IT IS
ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. It is a classical statistical model designed specifically for time series data. It has three components:
  AR (AutoRegressive, order p): Uses the relationship between a value and its own past values. "Today's price depends on the last p prices."
  I (Integrated, order d): Applies differencing to make the series stationary. A stationary series has constant mean and variance over time. d=1 means: work with (price_today - price_yesterday) instead of raw prices. This removes trends.
  MA (Moving Average, order q): Models the relationship between a value and past forecast errors. "Today's price depends on the error we made predicting yesterday's price."
Together: ARIMA(p, d, q) captures trend, autocorrelation, and error patterns in a unified model.

4.2.2 THE MATHEMATICS
After differencing d times, the series becomes y't.
The ARIMA model is:
  y't = c + phi_1*y't-1 + phi_2*y't-2 + ... + phi_p*y't-p + theta_1*e_t-1 + theta_2*e_t-2 + ... + theta_q*e_t-q + e_t
Where:
  y't      = differenced series at time t
  phi_i    = AR coefficients (learned from data)
  theta_j  = MA coefficients (learned from data)
  e_t      = white noise error at time t
  c        = constant term

AIC (Akaike Information Criterion):
  AIC = 2k - 2*ln(L)
  Where k = number of parameters, L = maximum likelihood
  Lower AIC = better model (penalizes complexity)
  Used in grid search to find best (p, d, q) combination.

4.2.3 HOW IT IS USED IN THIS PROJECT
In arima_model.py:
  - No scaling — raw prices used directly
  - _find_best_order tries all combinations: p in [1,2,3,4,5], d in [0,1], q in [0,1,2] = 30 combinations
  - Best order selected by lowest AIC on training data only
  - Rolling forecast (walk-forward validation):
      history starts as training data
      for each test point (capped at 60):
        fit ARIMA on history
        predict 1 step ahead
        append actual value to history (not prediction)
        this simulates real-world day-by-day prediction
  - Final forecast: fit ARIMA on full dataset, forecast N steps ahead

Code snippet from models/arima_model.py:
```python
    def _find_best_order(self, train: np.ndarray) -> tuple:
        best_aic   = np.inf
        best_order = (1, 1, 0)
        for p in range(1, 6):
            for d in range(0, 2):
                for q in range(0, 3):
                    try:
                        aic = ARIMA(train, order=(p, d, q)).fit().aic
                        if aic < best_aic:
                            best_aic   = aic
                            best_order = (p, d, q)
                    except Exception:
                        continue
        return best_order
```

4.2.4 WORKED EXAMPLE
Small dataset of 8 AAPL-style prices:
  Day 1: 226.43
  Day 2: 224.18
  Day 3: 220.79
  Day 4: 221.55
  Day 5: 221.89
  Day 6: 225.77
  Day 7: 229.04
  Day 8: 227.63

Use ARIMA(1,1,0) for simplicity.

Step 1 — Differencing (d=1):
  Compute first differences (removes trend):
  d1 = 224.18 - 226.43 = -2.25
  d2 = 220.79 - 224.18 = -3.39
  d3 = 221.55 - 220.79 =  0.76
  d4 = 221.89 - 221.55 =  0.34
  d5 = 225.77 - 221.89 =  3.88
  d6 = 229.04 - 225.77 =  3.27
  d7 = 227.63 - 229.04 = -1.41
  The differenced series: [-2.25, -3.39, 0.76, 0.34, 3.88, 3.27, -1.41]

Step 2 — AR(1) model on differenced series:
  Fit: d_t = phi_1 * d_t-1 + e_t
  Estimate phi_1 from data. Approximate by hand: phi_1 ≈ -0.3
  (negative means: if yesterday went down, today tends to go up)

Step 3 — Predict d8 (the next difference):
  d8_hat = phi_1 * d7 = -0.3 * (-1.41) = +0.423

Step 4 — Convert back to price:
  price_8 = price_7 + d8_hat = 227.63 + 0.423 = 228.05
  Actual Day 8 = 227.63
  Error = |227.63 - 228.05| = 0.42

Step 5 — Rolling forecast in practice:
  history = [226.43, 224.18, 220.79, 221.55, 221.89] (train)
  Predict Day 6 using ARIMA fit on history → pred_6
  Append actual 225.77 to history (not pred_6)
  Predict Day 7 using updated history → pred_7
  Append actual 229.04 to history
  Predict Day 8 using updated history → pred_8
  This walk-forward approach gives more honest error estimates than predicting the whole test set at once.

4.2.5 STRENGTHS AND WEAKNESSES
Strengths: Often outperforms advanced models for 1-step ahead stock prediction because it explicitly handles non-stationarity and relies on immediate previous states.
Weaknesses: Slow due to refitting in the rolling forecast.

4.3 LSTM
------------------------------------------------------

4.3.1 WHAT IT IS
LSTM stands for Long Short-Term Memory. It is a type of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) designed to learn patterns in sequential data.
The problem with standard RNNs: They suffer from the vanishing gradient problem — gradients shrink to near-zero during backpropagation through many time steps, so the model cannot learn long-range dependencies.
How LSTM solves this: LSTM introduces a cell state — a separate memory channel that runs through the entire sequence. Information can be written to, read from, or erased from the cell state using three gates:
  Forget Gate: Decides what fraction of the previous cell state to keep. Output near 0: forget. Near 1: keep.
  Input Gate: Decides what new information to write into the cell state.
  Output Gate: Decides what part of the cell state to output as h_t.
Why this works for stock prices: LSTM can learn that a pattern 30 days ago is still relevant to today's price — something linear models cannot capture.

4.3.2 THE MATHEMATICS
Given input x_t and previous hidden state h_t-1:
  Forget gate:    f_t = sigmoid(W_f [h_t-1; x_t] + b_f)
  Input gate:     i_t = sigmoid(W_i [h_t-1; x_t] + b_i)
  Candidate:      g_t = tanh(W_g [h_t-1; x_t] + b_g)
  Output gate:    o_t = sigmoid(W_o [h_t-1; x_t] + b_o)
  Cell state:     c_t = f_t * c_t-1 + i_t * g_t
  Hidden state:   h_t = o_t * tanh(c_t)
  Prediction:     y_hat = W_dense * h_T + b_dense

Training uses Adam optimizer minimizing Mean Squared Error:
Loss = (1/n) * sum((y_actual - y_predicted)^2)

Adam update rule:
m_t = beta1 * m_t-1 + (1 - beta1) * gradient
v_t = beta2 * v_t-1 + (1 - beta2) * gradient^2
w   = w - lr * m_t / (sqrt(v_t) + epsilon)
Default: lr=0.001, beta1=0.9, beta2=0.999

4.3.3 HOW IT IS USED IN THIS PROJECT
In lstm_model.py:
  - look_back = 60 (sequence length)
  - scaler.fit_transform on training data only
  - _make_sequences creates X of shape (n, 60, 1) and y of shape (n,)
  - Architecture:
      LSTM(50 units, return_sequences=True, input_shape=(60,1))
      Dropout(0.2)  — randomly zeros 20% of neurons to prevent overfit
      LSTM(50 units, return_sequences=False)
      Dropout(0.2)
      Dense(25)
      Dense(1)      — single price output
  - Compiled: optimizer=Adam, loss=MSE
  - Trained: epochs=20, batch_size=32, EarlyStopping(patience=5)
  - Fixed seeds: Python 42, NumPy 42, TensorFlow 42
  - Predictions inverse_transformed before metrics
  - Iterative forecast with ±1.5% per-step clamp to prevent drift

Code snippet from models/lstm_model.py:
```python
        model = Sequential([
            LSTM(50, return_sequences=True, input_shape=(x_train.shape[1], 1)),
            Dropout(0.2),
            LSTM(50, return_sequences=False),
            Dropout(0.2),
            Dense(25),
            Dense(1)
        ])
        model.compile(optimizer='adam', loss='mean_squared_error')

        es = EarlyStopping(monitor='loss', patience=5, restore_best_weights=True)
        model.fit(x_train, y_train, batch_size=32, epochs=20, callbacks=[es], verbose=0)
```

4.3.4 WORKED EXAMPLE
Sequence of 7 AAPL-style scaled prices (already scaled):
  t=1: 0.683
  t=2: 0.411
  t=3: 0.000
  t=4: 0.092
  t=5: 0.133
  t=6: 0.603
  t=7: 1.000

Use sequence_length = 3. Predict t=4 using [t=1, t=2, t=3].

Step 1 — Input sequence:
  x = [0.683, 0.411, 0.000]

Step 2 — Processing t=1 (x_1 = 0.683):
  Assume: h_0 = 0, c_0 = 0 (initial states)
  Weights (simplified for demo): W_f = 0.5, W_i = 0.4, W_g = 0.6, W_o = 0.5

  Forget gate: f_1 = sigmoid(0.5 * 0.683) = sigmoid(0.342) = 0.585
  Input gate:  i_1 = sigmoid(0.4 * 0.683) = sigmoid(0.273) = 0.568
  Candidate:   g_1 = tanh(0.6 * 0.683)   = tanh(0.410)    = 0.388
  Output gate: o_1 = sigmoid(0.5 * 0.683) = sigmoid(0.342) = 0.585
  Cell state:  c_1 = 0.585 * 0 + 0.568 * 0.388 = 0.220
  Hidden:      h_1 = 0.585 * tanh(0.220) = 0.585 * 0.218  = 0.128

Step 3 — Processing t=2 (x_2 = 0.411):
  h_1 = 0.128 feeds into the gates along with x_2.
  Forget gate: f_2 = sigmoid(0.5 * (0.128 + 0.411)) = sigmoid(0.270) = 0.567
  Input gate:  i_2 = sigmoid(0.4 * (0.128 + 0.411)) = sigmoid(0.216) = 0.554
  Candidate:   g_2 = tanh(0.6 * (0.128 + 0.411))   = tanh(0.323)    = 0.313
  Output gate: o_2 = sigmoid(0.5 * (0.128 + 0.411)) = sigmoid(0.270) = 0.567
  Cell state:  c_2 = 0.567 * 0.220 + 0.554 * 0.313 = 0.125 + 0.173  = 0.298
  Hidden:      h_2 = 0.567 * tanh(0.298) = 0.567 * 0.291 = 0.165

Step 4 — Processing t=3 (x_3 = 0.000):
  h_2 = 0.165 carries memory of the declining sequence.

4.3.5 STRENGTHS AND WEAKNESSES
Strengths: Captures non-linear relationships in price history. Learns long-range dependencies (60-day context window). Handles sequences naturally without feature engineering. Dropout layers provide regularization against overfitting.
Weaknesses: Requires more data to train well than LR or ARIMA. Computationally expensive (slowest of the three). Stochastic — results vary across runs without fixed seeds. Iterative forecasting accumulates errors over long horizons. Treats all 60 past days equally — no built-in seasonality.

Differences and why ARIMA > LR > LSTM for pure stock prediction:

Even though LSTM is a highly advanced Neural Network, stock market prediction on small datasets operates under a different set of rules than other domains. Here is why simpler models like ARIMA and Linear Regression (LR) often outperform it here:

1. Handling of Non-Stationarity: 
   Financial data is mostly a random walk with changing variance. ARIMA's integration step (differencing) natively handles this, automatically stripping away arbitrary upward or downward trends to find repeating patterns. LR and LSTM, especially when constrained by standard MinMax scaling (which squashes to [0,1]), severely struggle when future data inevitably breaks outside the historical minimum and maximum boundaries seen in training.

2. Short-term vs Long-term memory logic:
   In an efficient market, tomorrow's stock price is overwhelmingly dependent on today's price (recent momentum). ARIMA heavily localizes its focus on the immediate previous step and the immediate recent error. LSTM tries to find deep, complex patterns spanning the entire 60-day window, which too often amounts to fitting heavily to noise in financial time series. LR sits in the middle, looking at a 30-day window but assigning static, easily interpreted linear weights to recent days.

3. The Overfitting Trap:
   LSTM is a high variance, high-capacity model perfectly suited for rich, dense, and stationary datasets (like NLP, image processing, or translation where millions of data points exist). On small datasets of only 500 rows (2 years) of extremely noisy stock data, LSTM tends to overfit its capacity catastrophically despite dropout. It memorizes the training data perfectly but fails to generalize. Meanwhile, ARIMA and simple Linear Regression have extremely few parameters (e.g., 30 weights for LR, 2-5 coefficients for ARIMA) making them rigid but far harder to overfit to the noise.

Ultimately:
ARIMA > LR > LSTM because when the signal-to-noise ratio is extremely low (as in stocks), rigid low-capacity models (ARIMA, LR) effectively refuse to learn the noise, while flexible high-capacity models (LSTM) eagerly memorize it to their own detriment.
